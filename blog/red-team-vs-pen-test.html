Preparing for a red team test: a one-page checklist

Help your next test land cleanly by getting scope, expectations, and logistics aligned before anyone runs a single payload.

Why this distinction matters

“We need a red team” and “We need a penetration test” are phrases that get used interchangeably—but they describe very different types of work. Picking the wrong one can waste budget, confuse stakeholders, and leave real gaps untouched.

This guide is written for security leaders, IT directors, and CTOs who need to choose the right approach (and explain that choice to others).

What is a penetration test?

A penetration test is a focused, time-boxed assessment of systems, applications, or networks. The goal is to identify and validate vulnerabilities, then deliver clear findings and remediation guidance.

  • Scope: clearly defined (apps, IP ranges, APIs, cloud accounts).
  • Objective: find exploitable weaknesses and demonstrate impact.
  • Style: typically collaborative; testers talk to your team.
  • Outputs: list of findings, risk ratings, and recommended fixes.
  • Best for: improving technical security posture and hygiene.

What is a red team?

A red team engagement is an adversary emulation exercise. Instead of asking “what vulnerabilities exist?”, it asks “how would a real attacker try to achieve their goals against us?” and “how well would we detect and respond?”

  • Scope: broader, often objective-based (“obtain X data”, “persist in Y environment”).
  • Objective: test people, processes, and technology together.
  • Style: often stealthier; usually only a small group is aware.
  • Outputs: narratives of attack paths, detection gaps, and response performance.
  • Best for: mature programs that want to validate detection/response.
Short version: penetration tests focus on finding vulnerabilities. Red teams focus on using them (and everything else) to exercise your whole defense.

Key differences at a glance

Dimension Penetration test Red team engagement
Primary goal Find and validate vulnerabilities in defined scope. Test the end-to-end ability to detect and respond to real attacks.
Scope Specific systems, apps, networks, environments. Objective-based; may span multiple systems and attack paths.
Stealth Usually lower; collaboration with defenders is common. Often higher; limited awareness to simulate real adversary behavior.
Duration Typically 1–4 weeks. Often 4–12+ weeks, depending on depth and objectives.
Outputs Findings list, exploits, remediation guidance. Attack narratives, detection/response evaluation, improvement plan.
Prerequisites Basic logging and remediation capacity. Established security operations and incident response processes.

How to decide what you actually need

Use the questions below to guide the conversation with your team and leadership.

You probably want a penetration test if:

  • You have new or significantly changed systems (apps, APIs, infra, cloud).
  • You need to reduce known technical risk and get a prioritized list of fixes.
  • Your logging and detection are limited or still being built.
  • Remediation capacity is already stretched—so you need clear, focused findings.
  • You’re working toward compliance requirements that call for technical testing.

You might be ready for a red team if:

  • You already perform penetration tests on a regular basis.
  • You have a security operations center (SOC) or team actively monitoring alerts.
  • You want to understand detection gaps and response performance, not just vulnerabilities.
  • You’re asking questions like “Could someone quietly persist in our environment for months?”
  • Leadership is prepared to act on narrative, scenario-based outcomes—not a long list of CVEs.

A third option: collaborative purple-team exercises

There’s also a middle tier: purple-team exercises, where offensive and defensive teams work together in near real-time.

  • Red-team style techniques, but openly coordinated with defenders.
  • Each tactic is paired with a discussion: “Did we detect this? If not, how can we?”
  • Great for building shared understanding and improving detection content quickly.

For many organizations, a structured purple-team series is a better first step than a pure stealth red team.

How to explain the choice to leadership

When briefing non-technical stakeholders, keep it outcome-focused:

  • If we choose a penetration test: “We’ll get a prioritized list of weaknesses in <scope>, understand their impact, and have clear fixes for engineering.”
  • If we choose a red team: “We’ll learn how a realistic attacker would try to achieve specific goals and how well our people, processes, and tools respond.”

Both are valuable, but in different ways. The right choice is the one that matches your current maturity, capacity, and the decisions you need to make in the next 6–12 months.
