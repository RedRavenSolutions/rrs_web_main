Why you need a better read-out

A great penetration test or red team loses impact if the executive read-out feels like a bug report. Leaders don’t need payload details; they need a clear story that connects risk to decisions and next steps.

This template is a simple slide structure you can adapt to your organization and testing style. Think of it as a narrative spine rather than a rigid deck.

Slide 1 – Title & quick context

  • Title: “<Engagement type> results – <System/Org Name>”
  • Subtitle: Dates, type of testing (e.g., “External & internal pen test”).
  • Who’s in the room: Security, IT/eng leads, key stakeholders.
  • One-sentence purpose: “We’re here to show what we found, what it means, and what we recommend doing next.”

Slide 2 – Objectives and scope (one slide)

Anchor everyone before diving into findings.

  • Objectives: 2–3 bullets describing what you set out to learn.
    • “Evaluate external attack surface for critical internet-facing assets.”
    • “Assess lateral movement risk after a single endpoint compromise.”
  • Scope highlights: a simple table or bullets:
    • In-scope apps, environments, or business units.
    • What was out of scope (if important to mention).

Slide 3 – High-level outcome (the “so what” slide)

This is the slide executives will remember. Aim for a one-slide summary.

  • Overall assessment: short phrase (e.g., “Meaningful weaknesses, but clear path to improve”).
  • 3–5 key takeaways:
    • “We found X critical issues affecting Y systems.”
    • “The most realistic attack path looked like <short description>.”
    • “Detection worked well / needs improvement in <areas>.”
    • “We have a prioritized 30/60/90-day plan to address gaps.”

Slide 4 – Risk picture (visual)

Use a simple, honest visual—no need to overcomplicate.

  • Options:
    • Heatmap of critical/high/medium issues across major areas (identity, network, app, cloud).
    • Bar chart: number of findings by severity.
    • Simple table: “Top 3 risk themes” with brief descriptions.
  • Goal: show where risk clusters, not every single finding.

Slide 5 – Attack path / scenario (for red team or deeper tests)

When applicable, walk through a single primary attack path as a story.

  • Start with initial access (e.g., phishing, exposed service).
  • Show 3–5 major steps:
    • Initial foothold → privilege escalation → lateral movement → objective.
  • Note where:
    • Detection worked.
    • Detection did not work but should have.

Keep technical detail in the appendix. For executives, focus on what happened, why it was possible, and what you’re going to do about it.

Slide 6 – Key strengths and positives

Don’t skip this. It builds trust and shows progress.

  • Highlight 3–4 strengths:
    • Areas where configurations, controls, or processes worked as intended.
    • Detections that fired at the right time.
    • Teams that responded quickly or thoroughly.

Slide 7 – Top risks and themes

Group related issues into themes that make sense to leadership.

  • Examples:
    • “Identity and access management gaps”
    • “Lack of segmentation between critical systems”
    • “Inconsistent patching and hardening in <area>”
  • For each theme: one sentence on impact and one on cause.

Slide 8 – Remediation plan (30/60/90)

Turn findings into a time-boxed plan instead of a giant backlog.

  • 30 days: quick wins and critical stop-the-bleeding fixes.
  • 60 days: structural changes for the most important gaps.
  • 90+ days: deeper improvements, refactors, or roadmap items.

Assign owners at the team level, not individuals (e.g., “Identity team”, “AppSec”, “Platform eng”).

Slide 9 – Support needed from leadership

This is your chance to make explicit asks while you have attention.

  • Examples:
    • “Endorse and prioritize the 30/60/90-day plan.”
    • “Support temporary change freezes where needed to implement fixes safely.”
    • “Align on acceptable risk for areas we won’t address this quarter.”

Slide 10 – Q&A and next steps

  • Summarize:
    • What we tested
    • What we found
    • What we’re doing about it
  • Invite questions from leadership and clarify any trade-offs.

Appendix – Where the technical detail lives

The executive deck shouldn’t hold every payload and PoC—that belongs in the technical report and appendix. Consider including:

  • Per-finding technical details, evidence, and replication steps.
  • Mappings to frameworks (e.g., MITRE ATT&CK, OWASP categories).
  • Any logs, timelines, or packet captures relevant to detections.

Share the detailed report and appendix with the right technical teams, but keep the main read-out tight and decision-focused.
