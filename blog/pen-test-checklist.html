Preparing for a penetration test: a one-page checklist

Help your next test land cleanly by getting scope, expectations, and logistics aligned before anyone runs a single payload.

Why this checklist exists

A penetration test goes sideways long before the first payload fires. Misaligned expectations, unclear scope, or last-minute surprises can turn a valuable exercise into noise. This checklist is designed for security and IT leads who want a clean, predictable, and useful test.

Use it as a 15–20 minute prep exercise with your internal stakeholders before you lock in dates and scope with any testing partner.

1. Clarify objectives and success criteria

Before talking tools or techniques, get clear on why you’re testing.

  • Primary objective: What question are you trying to answer?
    • “How hard is it to breach our external perimeter?”
    • “What can an attacker do after phishing a single user?”
    • “How exposed is our new customer-facing app?”
  • Success looks like:
    • We have a prioritized list of weaknesses and clear fixes.
    • We understand realistic attack paths, not just individual bugs.
    • We can brief leadership with a concise, business-friendly summary.
  • Constraints: Time, budget, change freeze windows, compliance drivers.
Quick sanity check: if you can’t describe the test in one sentence (“We’re testing <this> so we can decide <that>”), you probably need to refine the objective.

2. Define scope and rules of engagement

Scope is where most confusion happens. Document it explicitly and share it with everyone affected.

  • In scope:
    • Applications, APIs, domains, IP ranges, environments (prod / non-prod).
    • Cloud accounts, VPN portals, SSO, admin panels.
  • Out of scope:
    • Systems that are too fragile or regulated for testing right now.
    • 3rd-party hosted services where you lack permission.
  • Rules of engagement:
    • Testing windows / maintenance windows.
    • Whether social engineering or phishing is allowed.
    • Any “no-go” techniques (e.g., DoS, destructive payloads).

3. Align stakeholders and communication

Few things cause more friction than a surprised stakeholder. Make sure the right people know what’s happening.

  • Internal owner: who is accountable for this test?
  • Key contacts: security, IT/ops, app owners, helpdesk.
  • Communication channels:
    • Primary channel: email, Teams/Slack, or a shared ticket.
    • How to escalate if something looks like a real incident.
  • Change freezes: avoid scheduling tests during major releases or migrations.
Pro tip: tell the helpdesk you’re running a test. Otherwise, they may treat scanner traffic as a live incident and escalate in all the wrong ways.

4. Prepare the environment

A test against a broken environment doesn’t tell you much. Stabilize the basics first.

  • Ensure key systems in scope are up and reachable.
  • Resolve any known, easy issues (expired certs, obviously default creds).
  • Confirm that monitoring/logging is functioning for:
    • Perimeter / WAF / firewall logs
    • Endpoint detection and response (EDR)
    • Authentication logs (SSO, VPN, AD/IdP)

5. Decide how “noisy” you want the test to be

Different goals require different levels of stealth and collaboration.

  • Collaborative (purple-team style):
    • Testers and defenders work together in near real-time.
    • Goal: improve detection/response and learn quickly.
  • Black-box / limited knowledge:
    • Only a few stakeholders know about the test.
    • Goal: simulate a more realistic attacker viewpoint.

Be clear about which style you want—your partner will adjust tactics and comms accordingly.

6. Access, test data, and accounts

Some tests require credentials or special accounts. Sorting this out ahead of time avoids painful delays.

  • Provide test accounts where appropriate:
    • Different roles (user, admin, read-only) as needed by scope.
  • Decide whether production data is acceptable, or if you need masked / non-prod environments.
  • Ensure any VPN or SSO access required for internal testing is ready.

7. Legal, approvals, and documentation

Make sure the right paperwork is in place before anything starts.

  • Confirm contracts / statements of work are signed.
  • Ensure a documented authorization letter exists and is accessible.
  • Align with internal legal or compliance, especially in regulated industries.

8. Plan for the read-out and follow-through

The value of a penetration test is realized after the report lands.

  • Schedule a read-out session with:
    • Security and engineering leads
    • Relevant product/IT owners
    • At least one executive or decision-maker, if possible
  • Decide who owns the remediation plan and tracking.
  • Agree on how and when you’ll retest critical items.

Printable one-page checklist

Use this as a quick “pre-flight” before your next engagement:

  • [ ] We can state the objective of the test in one sentence.
  • [ ] We have a written scope (in, out, rules of engagement).
  • [ ] Key stakeholders know the dates and what to expect.
  • [ ] Monitoring and logging are working for in-scope systems.
  • [ ] Test accounts and access paths are ready.
  • [ ] Contracts and written authorization are in place.
  • [ ] A read-out session is on the calendar with the right people.

Run through this a week before the test starts, and again the day before. Your future self will thank you.
